 II. Weaponizing Probability

The specific tactical application of Theron's behavioral model required a level of real-time intelligence integration that no previous mortal military force had attempted: the model's predictions were only as good as the current situation variables it was processing. Stale information produced stale predictions. The tactical value of knowing what the Seraphic command would do in the next twelve minutes degraded rapidly after those twelve minutes.

Eight-minute intelligence cycles. The model's predictions, therefore, were reliable approximately six of those eight minutes â€” two minutes before the new cycle's data would potentially shift the prediction.

He ran the entire coalition's strategic operation on six-minute prediction windows.

The commanders who worked under him described this in their post-war accounts as the most cognitively demanding environment they had ever operated in: they received orders that were specific to the next several minutes and that required execution within the prediction window's remaining time. Orders were not standing orders. They were time-stamped. He would countermand a standing order when the new cycle's data changed the prediction.

Some commanders adapted to this. Some did not. Theron tracked the adaptation rate and routed his time-critical instructions through the commanders whose execution reliability at the six-minute window was highest.
